CT — Head
Community Referred Radiology

Head
For suspected TIA within 7 days: completion of the BPAC TIA decision support tool is required to confirm CT Head appropriate. For first seizure, MRI is the preferred first-line investigation where available.
Acute — within 48 hoursAcute 48hr
  • Suspected TIA — within 7 days of episode, AND CT +/- CTA recommended by HNZ neurologist/stroke specialist OR BPAC TIA decision support tool indicates CT appropriate OR patient unable to access rapid specialist care
  • Focal neurological signs
  • Seizure in a palliative care setting and result may change management
  • Secondary care clinician or PCRL or radiologist advises referral for same or next day CT scan
P2 — Urgent, within 2 weeksP2 — 2 wks
  • Change in pattern of headaches with progressive increase in frequency or severity (especially aged 50+) AND associated with chronic illness with potential cerebral complications (especially malignancy), OR exacerbated by coughing/sneezing/Valsalva (not typical migraine), OR associated with persistent nausea/vomiting (not typical migraine), OR objective neurological deficit
  • Cognitive impairment with one or more features suggesting space-occupying lesion, bleed, or hydrocephalus: rapid cognitive decline over weeks; focal neurological signs; history of falls; anticoagulant therapy; gait ataxia or incontinence; history of cancers with propensity to brain metastases (lung, melanoma, breast)
  • First seizure aged 21 years or older — clear history, patient low risk, OR MRI not available
  • Secondary care clinician or PCRL or radiologist advises referral for urgent CT scan
P4 — Deferrable, within 6–12 weeksP4 — 6–12 wks
  • Cognitive impairment without features suggesting SOL, bleed, or hydrocephalus — and establishing the subtype diagnosis will alter management
  • Secondary care clinician or PCRL or radiologist advises referral for non-urgent CT scan
Not funded / out of criteria: Established advanced dementia >2 years, no unexpected change, subtype will not alter management — not routinely funded. Seizures with risk factors for intracranial pathology — seek Neurology phone advice rather than CT.
Alternative management: Seizures with risk factors for intracranial pathology — seek Neurology phone advice. Poorly controlled or recurrent seizures — follow the appropriate pathway (Epilepsy in Adults, Epilepsy in Women, First Seizure in Adults, Seizure in Palliative Care HealthPathways).

Chest/Abdomen/Pelvis
Reserve for patients where after initial investigations the GP remains strongly suspicious of malignancy but no focal pathology or biopsy site is identified. Two or more persistently abnormal bloods (Hb, CRP, Ca, ALP) significantly increases probability of cancer.
P2 — Urgent, within 2 weeksP2 — 2 wks
  • Male >50 or female >60 with strong suspicion of malignancy, no focal pathology, AND unintentional unexplained weight loss >5% over 3–6 months (+/- abdominal pain, fatigue, nausea)
  • Male >40 or female >50 with strong suspicion of malignancy, no focal pathology, AND weight loss >5% over 3–6 months AND 2+ of: raised CRP, low Hb, raised Ca, high platelets, high ALP, low albumin — persistent on repeat after 3 weeks
  • Secondary care clinician, PCRL or radiologist advises referral for urgent CT Chest, Abdomen and Pelvis
Not funded / out of criteria: Not indicated where: current cancer under follow-up; investigated by secondary care within 12 months for same symptoms; localising features on initial investigations; recent USS abdomen/pelvis within 3 months; CT CAP within 12 months.
Alternative management: Not indicated where: patient has a current cancer diagnosis and is under continued follow-up; patient has been investigated by secondary care within the last 12 months for the same symptoms/signs; presentation requires urgent admission or urgent secondary care assessment; localising clinical features or results from preliminary investigations suggest cancer in a specific system; recent ultrasound of abdomen and pelvis within the last three months (seek advice from radiologist or secondary care specialist); CT Chest, Abdomen and Pelvis within the last 12 months (seek advice from radiologist or secondary care specialist).

CT — Chest
Community Referred Radiology
Select the anatomical site(s) being requested, then complete the clinical indicators for each site. Review the National Community Referral Criteria for Imaging and HealthPathways before referring. View HealthPathways ›
Chest
Urgent CXR (same or next day) remains the initial investigation where lung cancer is suspected. CT Chest is a subsequent step. A normal CXR does not exclude lung cancer (sensitivity ~75–80%).
P2 — Urgent, within 2 weeksP2 — 2 wks
  • Unexplained haemoptysis (in absence of chest infection, upper respiratory tract causes excluded) AND investigations including CXR fail to identify a cause
  • Aged over 55 years, current smoker, AND one or more new onset concerning symptoms for lung cancer persistent for 6 weeks despite appropriate treatment AND unexplained despite CXR and bloods
  • Indeterminate abnormality on CXR raising possibility of lung cancer (e.g. bulky hilum) AND CT chest recommended in radiology report
  • Persistent consolidation on 6-week follow-up CXR AND CT chest recommended in radiology report
  • Secondary care clinician or radiologist advises urgent staging CT chest/liver for suspicious lesion on CXR AND CT is advised next step on local HealthPathway
  • Secondary care clinician, PCRL or radiologist advises referral for urgent CT chest
P3 — Non-deferrable, within 6 weeksP3 — 6 wks
  • Isolated pulmonary nodule identified on CXR, cannot be confirmed as benign from x-ray appearances alone, AND referral for CT chest recommended in radiology report
  • Secondary care clinician, PCRL or radiologist advises referral for non-urgent CT chest
Not funded / out of criteria: Initial presentation with respiratory symptoms: refer for urgent CXR first. Re-presentation with normal CXR but not meeting CT criteria: seek advice from secondary care respiratory team.
Alternative management: Patient re-presents with concerning symptoms and CXR is normal but doesn't meet CT criteria — seek advice from secondary care respiratory team regarding appropriate management and whether CT chest is warranted. Persistent non-specific non-localising symptoms concerning for underlying cancer (e.g. weight loss, fatigue) and initial investigations including CXR and bloods fail to identify a cause — see CT Chest/Abdomen/Pelvis HealthPathway. Initial presentation with respiratory symptoms or signs concerning for lung cancer — refer for urgent CXR as the initial investigation. Follow-up of nodule discovered incidentally on CT chest scan — refer to secondary care for management advice unless radiology report confirms benign.

Colonography (CTC)
CTC may be appropriate where the patient is over 80, has co-morbidities making colonoscopy higher risk, has an abdominal mass, or following failed/incomplete colonoscopy. All referrals should go via secondary care single point of entry for triage to colonoscopy or CTC.
P2 — Urgent, within 2 weeksP2 — 2 wks
  • Altered bowel habit (looser and/or more frequent) >6 weeks PLUS unexplained rectal bleeding (benign anal causes treated or excluded), aged >50 years
  • Unexplained rectal bleeding (benign anal causes treated or excluded) WITH iron deficiency anaemia (Hb below reference range AND low ferritin)
  • Known or suspected colorectal cancer (on imaging, palpable, or visible on rectal examination) — for pre-operative procedure to rule out synchronous pathology
  • Secondary care clinician or radiologist advises referral for urgent CTC
P3 — Non-deferrable, within 6 weeksP3 — 6 wks
  • Altered bowel habit (looser and/or more frequent) >6 weeks, aged 50 years or older
  • Altered bowel habit >6 weeks PLUS unexplained rectal bleeding (benign anal causes treated or excluded), aged 40-50 years
  • Unexplained rectal bleeding (benign anal causes treated or excluded), aged 50 years or older
  • Unexplained iron deficiency anaemia (Hb below local reference range AND low ferritin)
  • NZGG Category 2 family history PLUS altered bowel habit >6 weeks PLUS unexplained rectal bleeding, aged 40 years or older
  • NZGG Category 3 family history PLUS altered bowel habit >6 weeks PLUS unexplained rectal bleeding, aged 25 years or older
  • Secondary care clinician or radiologist advises referral for non-urgent CTC
Not funded / out of criteria: Not indicated for: acute diarrhoea <6 weeks (likely infectious); IBS; constipation as single symptom; uncomplicated CT-proven diverticulitis; abdominal pain alone; low ferritin with normal Hb aged <50. All criteria require ability to tolerate bowel prep AND no colonoscopy/CTC within last 5 years (unless clear indication).
Alternative management: Rectal bleeding aged less than 50 years (normal haemoglobin) — consider secondary care referral for assessment and possible flexible sigmoidoscopy.

IVU / CT Renal
CT IVU is preferred for renal masses, painless haematuria, and obstruction. Contraindicated if eGFR <30 or iodinated contrast allergy — use renal ultrasound instead. CT KUB is a separate non-contrast scan for stone detection.
P2 — Urgent, within 2 weeksP2 — 2 wks
  • Macroscopic haematuria where UTI excluded (or persists after treating UTI) AND patient aged 40-85 years
  • Macroscopic haematuria where UTI excluded AND patient aged under 40, initial investigations including renal ultrasound normal, and haematuria persists
  • Secondary care clinician, PCRL or radiologist advises referral for urgent CT IVU or CT Renal scan
P3 — Non-deferrable, within 6 weeksP3 — 6 wks
  • CT IVU recommended by secondary care specialist for unexplained asymptomatic microscopic haematuria after initial investigations including renal tract ultrasound fail to identify a cause
  • Multiphase renal CT recommended by secondary care specialist or radiology report for incidental finding on ultrasound: solid lesion >1cm OR complex cyst (Bosniak IIF, III, or IV)
  • Secondary care clinician, PCRL or radiologist advises referral for non-urgent CT IVU or CT Renal scan

KUB
For pregnant patients or females <35 without haematuria, fever, history of stones, or solitary kidney: request renal tract ultrasound instead. Consider plain X-ray KUB at same time. Avoid repeated CT KUB in otherwise healthy patients aged <50 with known stones and typical presentation without red flags.
Refer for acute assessment — red flags (admit, do not image)Emergency
  • Creatinine >160 mmol/L or eGFR <45 ml/min
  • Solitary kidney
  • Temperature above 38 degrees
  • Peritonitis / signs of sepsis
  • Known bilateral ureteric stones
Acute — within 48 hours (no red flags)Acute 48hr
  • First presentation of suspected renal colic with no red flags
  • Secondary care clinician, PCRL or radiologist advises referral for same or next day CT KUB
S2 — Specified date, within 2 weeks of target date
  • Follow-up per Renal Colic and Urinary Tract Stones HealthPathway, if calculus not visible on KUB X-ray
  • Secondary care clinician, PCRL or radiologist advises referral for specified-date CT KUB
Not funded / out of criteria: Not routinely funded for: recurrent renal colic in patients aged <50 with typical presentation, no red flags, and confirmed stone on CT KUB in last 5 years; surveillance of asymptomatic patients with previous nephrolithiasis; children younger than 16 years.
Alternative management: Females aged younger than 35 years with renal colic — arrange renal ultrasound rather than CT KUB, unless the patient has: haematuria x10, fever, history of stones, or solitary kidney. Pregnant women with renal colic — arrange renal ultrasound.

Sinus
CRS symptoms are poor predictors of sinus disease on imaging (specificity ~30%). Early CT sinus is more cost-efficient than presuming CRS on symptoms alone. All prerequisites below must be met.
P3 — Non-deferrable, within 6 weeksP3 — 6 wks
  • Suspected chronic rhinosinusitis AND moderate to severe symptoms persist despite daily nasal saline irrigation AND intranasal corticosteroids (INCS) trialled for at least 12 weeks with satisfactory patient technique and compliance AND patient has not previously had a CT sinus scan (mandatory)
  • ORL specialist, PCRL or radiologist advises referral for non-urgent CT sinus
Not funded / out of criteria: Not routinely funded for follow-up scans to assess treatment response. Red flags for sino-nasal malignancy (unilateral nasal obstruction with haemo-serous discharge; Horner syndrome/diplopia/cranial neuropathies; paraesthesia of cheek/gingiva/palate) — refer urgently to ORL.
Alternative management: Rhinosinusitis symptoms associated with any of the following red flags for sino-nasal malignancy — refer for urgent ORL assessment: unilateral nasal obstruction with haemo-serous discharge; recent onset of Horner syndrome, diplopia, or cranial neuropathies (particularly sensory change); paraesthesia in the skin of cheek, maxillary gingiva, or hard palate. Allergic rhinitis causing nasal obstruction or discharge — see local Rhino-Sinusitis HealthPathway.

Ultrasound — Abdomen
Community Referred Radiology
Select the anatomical site being requested, then complete the clinical indicators. Note: maternity and gynaecological ultrasound is outside CRR scope — refer via separate pathway. View HealthPathways ›
Abdomen
Opportunistic liver function testing is not indicated for asymptomatic people without risk factors.
Refer for acute assessment — without initial imagingEmergency
  • Suspected acute cholecystitis or cholangitis (intractable biliary colic not relieved by simple analgesia, especially with fever or persistent vomiting)
  • Acute abdomen, including possible appendicitis
  • Suspicion of ruptured abdominal aortic aneurysm
  • Suspected testicular torsion
Acute — within 48 hoursAcute 48hr
  • Painful jaundice
  • Painless jaundice without obvious cause (consider discussing with secondary care or PCRL — CT may be more appropriate)
  • Secondary care clinician, PCRL or radiologist advises referral for same or next day ultrasound scan
P2 — Urgent, within 2 weeksP2 — 2 wks
  • Obstructive picture on liver function testing: elevated ALP more than 2x ULN together with elevated GGT +/- raised bilirubin +/- raised ALT
  • Suspected acute cholecystitis/biliary colic with raised ALP and GGT +/- bilirubin AND symptoms settle with analgesia AND biliary tree has not previously been imaged
  • Secondary care clinician, PCRL or radiologist advises referral for urgent ultrasound scan
P3 — Non-deferrable, within 6 weeksP3 — 6 wks
  • Recurrent right upper quadrant or epigastric non-dyspepsia pain without previous imaging
  • Suspected resolved acute biliary colic or cholecystitis, without previous imaging showing gallstones, AND patient is a potential candidate for and would accept cholecystectomy
  • ALP greater than 2x ULN AND elevated GGT confirming liver origin AND elevated ALP persists on re-testing after 3 months
  • ALT greater than 120 IU/L AND conservative methods unsuccessful in patients with high risk factors (diabetes, obesity, hepatotoxic medications) AND no clinical features of concern
  • Isolated raised GGT more than 5x ULN and persists on re-testing after 3 months
  • Asymptomatic newly suspected abdominal aortic aneurysm (e.g. on clinical examination or other imaging)
  • Non-pulsatile abdominal mass and suspected enlarged liver on examination
  • Secondary care clinician, PCRL or radiologist advises referral for non-urgent ultrasound scan
S3 — Specified date, within 6 weeks of target date
  • HCC surveillance using liver ultrasound as initially recommended by a gastroenterologist or other secondary care specialist, or as recommended by an agreed local pathway
  • Surveillance of known abdominal aortic aneurysm according to local HealthPathway or NZ Vascular Society guidelines (2015) or secondary care specialist advice
  • Follow-up of incidental finding on imaging (including gallbladder polyps) according to Incidental Findings on Imaging HealthPathway or radiology report/PCRL advice
Not funded / out of criteria: Not routinely funded for: hernia in adults; infective hepatitis; follow-up of previously diagnosed gallstones in asymptomatic patients; isolated bloating; follow-up of simple renal or hepatic cysts; screening for AAA or fatty liver.
Alternative management: Upper abdominal non-pulsatile mass not consistent with hepatomegaly — discuss with PCRL or relevant secondary care specialist as referral for CT abdomen may be more appropriate. Unexplained weight loss, persistent fatigue, vague abdominal pain and suspicion of underlying malignancy — see local CT Chest, Abdomen and Pelvis HealthPathway.

Carotid
All patients with suspected TIA who after specialist assessment are candidates for carotid endarterectomy should have urgent carotid imaging. Choice of modality (US, CTA, or MRA) based on local expertise. Carotid imaging preferably completed at same visit as brain imaging.
Refer for acute assessment — without initial imagingEmergency
  • Suspected TIA with any of: atypical symptoms; currently on anticoagulants; new or known atrial fibrillation
Acute — within 48 hoursAcute 48hr
  • Suspected TIA with typical symptoms, not in AF, not on anticoagulants AND episode within last 2 weeks AND symptoms consistent with anterior circulation TIA (language dysfunction and/or limb weakness) AND patient is a candidate for carotid endarterectomy AND acute stroke specialist not available AND carotid imaging recommended by HNZ neurologist/stroke specialist OR BPAC TIA tool indicates carotid imaging appropriate
Not funded / out of criteria: Not funded for: asymptomatic bruits; carotid artery calcification on X-ray; screening tests. High risk TIAs (atypical symptoms, AF, anticoagulants) — request acute on-call stroke specialist assessment.
Alternative management: High-risk TIAs (atypical symptoms, patient in atrial fibrillation and on anticoagulants) — request acute on-call stroke specialist assessment. Suspected TIA over 7 days ago — see local TIA HealthPathway.

DVT
Use Well's score to stratify clinical suspicion. D-dimer should be used in conjunction with Well's score.
Acute — within 24 hoursAcute 24hr
  • Superficial venous thrombosis affecting the proximal long saphenous vein in the thigh
  • Clinically suspected upper limb DVT
  • Same day DVT US recommended by HNZ Haematology service or other secondary care specialist
Acute — within 48 hoursAcute 48hr
  • Clinically suspected lower limb DVT AND Well's score of 2 or greater
  • Clinically suspected lower limb DVT AND Well's score less than 2 AND positive D-dimer
S1 — Specified date, within 1 week of target date
  • No DVT seen on initial ultrasound but clinical suspicion remains high AND D-dimer positive AND high likelihood on Well's score (2 or higher) — follow-up scan 5-8 days after initial scan
  • Check for proximal clot extension in a calf DVT not being treated with anticoagulation, between 5-8 days after initial scan
S2 — Specified date, within 2 weeks of target date
  • Follow-up scan after anticoagulation therapy as recommended by secondary care specialist
Not funded / out of criteria: Not routinely funded for: routine follow-up scanning to confirm clot resolution; superficial venous thrombosis unless affecting the proximal long saphenous vein in the thigh.
Alternative management: Children aged younger than 15 years — see local DVT HealthPathway. Pregnant women — see local DVT HealthPathway.

Musculoskeletal (incl. Shoulder)
No routine direct access to publicly funded (non-ACC) musculoskeletal ultrasound. Ultrasound findings alone do not typically change management. Initial management should always include a 2-3 month trial of physiotherapy. Radiology is not a prerequisite for a trial of steroid injection when red flags are excluded.
P3-P4 — within 6 to 12 weeksP3 — 6 wks
  • Secondary care clinician or Primary Care Radiology Liaison or radiologist advises referral for non-urgent shoulder or another musculoskeletal ultrasound
Not funded / out of criteria: Not routinely funded for: Achilles or other tendon pain; plantar fasciitis; forefoot pain (suspected Morton's neuroma); tennis elbow; golfer's elbow; tenosynovitis; trochanteric pain (gluteal tendinopathy). If inadequate response to physiotherapy seek management advice from secondary care.
Alternative management: Shoulder pain with reduced passive range of motion and frozen shoulder is suspected — see local Shoulder Pain HealthPathway. Suspected olecranon or other bursitis — see local Shoulder Pain HealthPathway.

Community Referred Radiology

Neck / Thyroid
For palpable masses, FNA (preferably US-guided) provides better diagnostic accuracy than US alone and is the preferred initial diagnostic approach. Ultrasound reveals nodules in up to 65% of adults — the large majority are benign.
P2 — Urgent, within 2 weeksP2 — 2 wks
  • Palpable thyroid nodule with suspicious features (painless rapid growth over 2-3 months, unexplained voice changes, youth/child, family history of MEN syndrome, enlarged neck lymph nodes) AND local pathway or secondary care specialist recommends referral for ultrasound
  • FDG-avid incidental thyroid nodule on PET-CT scan of any size
  • Undifferentiated, solitary, non-pulsatile neck mass: present >3 weeks despite antibiotics AND larger than 1.5cm in diameter AND no obvious cause
  • Urgent Ultrasound Neck or Thyroid recommended by a secondary care clinician, PCRL, or radiologist
P3 — Non-deferrable, within 6 weeksP3 — 6 wks
  • Palpable thyroid nodule, no suspicious features, euthyroid — initial imaging
  • Non-urgent Ultrasound Neck or Thyroid recommended by a secondary care clinician, PCRL, or radiologist
P4 — Deferrable, within 6-12 weeksP4 — 6–12 wks
  • Solitary impalpable incidental thyroid nodule (ITN) on CT or MR imaging, TSH normal or elevated AND nodule >10mm in patient aged <35 years OR >15mm in patient aged 35+ years OR any size with adverse features on CT/MR imaging
  • Multiple incidental thyroid nodules on CT or MR imaging, TSH normal or elevated, AND one or more nodules 20mm or more in longest diameter
S2 — Specified date, within 2 weeks of target date
  • Non-urgent deferrable Ultrasound Neck or Thyroid recommended by a secondary care specialist, PCRL, or radiologist
  • Follow-up ultrasound recommended by a secondary care specialist or radiologist for a high-risk thyroid nodule previously identified on ultrasound, CT or MRI
Not funded / out of criteria: Not routinely funded for: ITN with co-morbidities and limited life expectancy; solitary ITN on CT/MR <10mm without adverse features; multiple nodules all <20mm without adverse features. Incidental thyroid nodule with reduced TSH — refer for nuclear medicine scan.
Alternative management: Multiple neck masses or suspected metastatic lymphadenopathy — investigate and refer as per the Neck Lumps in Adults HealthPathway. Incidental thyroid nodule on CT scan or MRI and TSH reduced — refer for nuclear medicine Tc-99m pertechnetate scan. Goitre or diffuse thyroid swelling with severe obstructive symptoms (stridor, wheezing at rest) — refer for acute secondary care assessment as per local HealthPathway. Goitre or thyroid mass with mild to moderate obstructive symptoms — see local Goitre HealthPathway. Thyrotoxicosis/Hyperthyroidism — see local Hyperthyroidism HealthPathway. Hypothyroidism — see local Hypothyroid HealthPathway. If presenting symptoms do not meet criteria but ultrasound may be indicated — seek advice from PCRL or relevant secondary care specialist.

Ultrasound — Pelvis
Community Referred Radiology

Pelvis
In acute pelvic pain, ultrasound as a rule-out investigation is limited — acuity of presentation should determine referral to hospital vs community management. PID is commonly associated with a normal ultrasound and is typically a clinical diagnosis. Maori and Pacific peoples are disproportionately affected by endometrial cancer — lower threshold for investigation.
Refer for acute assessment — without initial imagingEmergency
  • Clinically unstable patients with suspected gynaecologic or pelvic cause
Acute — within 24 hoursAcute 24hr
  • Sudden onset severe pelvic pain, clinically stable — suspected ovarian torsion or ovarian cyst accident (rupture or bleed)
  • Possible endometritis from RPOC — persistent pelvic pain and tenderness +/- low grade fever +/- bleeding, outside 2-week post-miscarriage maternity-funded scan window
  • IUCD strings missing or IUCD breaks on removal with only fragment removed AND symptoms suggestive of possible perforation (cramping pain, discharge, unexplained bleeding)
Acute — within 48 hoursAcute 48hr
  • Suspected ongoing pregnancy >14 days post abortion AND urine HCG >1000 IU at 14 days OR serial serum bHCG remains high/increased after EMA
  • Secondary care clinician, PCRL or radiologist advises referral for same or next day ultrasound pelvis
P2 — Urgent, within 2 weeksP2 — 2 wks
  • Pelvic mass on examination suspicious of cancer
  • Post-menopausal bleeding (vaginal bleeding after >12 months amenorrhoea around expected age of menopause approximately 50 years)
  • Persistent post-menopausal bleeding at any age while on continuous combined MHT and it has been >6 months since starting therapy
  • No palpable pelvic mass but new abdominal or pelvic symptoms on a persistent or frequent basis (>12 times/month) AND Ca-125 35 units/mL or greater irrespective of menopause status
  • AUB with non-uterine causes excluded AND woman of any age with AUB AND at least two risk factors for EH/EC OR pelvic mass suspected on examination with no prior imaging
  • Atypical (AG2) endometrial cells reported on cervical screening cytology in women of any age and menopause status
  • Secondary care clinician, PCRL or radiologist advises referral for urgent US pelvis
P3 — Non-deferrable, within 6 weeksP3 — 6 wks
  • Women aged 45 years or older with AUB and non-uterine causes excluded
  • Women aged younger than 45 years with AUB AND one or more risk factors for EH/EC
  • Women aged younger than 45 years with AUB who have not responded to first-line treatment trialled for at least 3 months
  • Perimenopausal woman on combined cyclical MHT with ongoing irregular bleeding or bleeding outside progestogen withdrawal, despite trial of increased progestogen, and it has been >6 months since starting MHT
  • Benign-appearing endometrial cells on cervical smear AND at least one of: post-menopausal; symptomatic; suspected mass on exam; 1+ EH/EC risk factor
  • Suspected ovarian cyst (unilateral pelvic pain >4 weeks and/or unilateral tenderness) or other pelvic mass and low/uncertain suspicion of cancer
  • Pelvic pain >6 weeks, unrelated to menstrual cycle, with PID excluded as cause per local PID HealthPathway
  • Primary amenorrhoea (absence of menarche by age 16 years, or age 14 years if absent secondary sexual characteristics)
  • Secondary dysmenorrhoea — new onset, debilitating pain persists despite 3-month trial of medical management
  • Secondary care clinician, PCRL or radiologist advises referral for routine ultrasound pelvis
P4 — Deferrable, within 6-12 weeksP4 — 6–12 wks
  • PCOS suspected but only one of three Rotterdam diagnostic criteria fulfilled (hyperandrogenism, polycystic ovaries, irregular menses)
  • Known fibroid >5cm which becomes symptomatic (pelvic pressure, back pain, dyspareunia, urination difficulties) after commencing MHT
  • Secondary care clinician, PCRL or radiologist advises referral for deferrable non-urgent ultrasound pelvis
Not funded / out of criteria: Not routinely funded for: routine follow-up of known fibroids; subfertility; follow-up of asymptomatic simple ovarian cyst (O-RADS 2) <3cm post-menopausal or <5cm pre-menopausal; screening for ovarian cancer family history (refer to secondary care). Women aged <45 with AUB, no risk factors, no abnormality on exam, and first-line treatment not yet trialled.
Alternative management: Suspected retained products of conception (RPOC) with persistent or heavy bleeding after abortion or miscarriage — see local Abortion HealthPathway or Miscarriage HealthPathway. Suspected endometriosis in the absence of a clinically palpable mass — see local Endometriosis HealthPathway. Primary dysmenorrhoea in the absence of a clinically palpable mass — see local Dysmenorrhoea HealthPathway. Pelvic inflammatory disease in the absence of a clinically palpable mass — see local PID HealthPathway. Cervical polyps — referral to secondary care rather than ultrasound is recommended for women with polyps which prove difficult to remove. Secondary amenorrhoea — pelvic USS is not normally helpful unless a pelvic mass is present or the patient has significantly raised androgens.
